Empire in Antigone

by Zachary Groz

History 2/18/18

Sophocles’ Antigone contends with assimilation and the constraints of culture. Sophocles’ culture is one determined by binary sets: order and chaos, state and individual, material law and divine ordinance, family and community, natural and human, internal and external. Antigone’s central conflict depends on the transformation from internal to external, from familiar to foreign.


Antigone’s opening lines introduce a reality of impending ruin. Oedipus’ tragic house retains the stain of incest and patricide.[1] Like the cultural transgressions of Oedipus, the political allegiances of Polynices portend immediate disaster for both the state and the family. Antigone, too, is instantly associated with criminality.[2] “There is nothing—no grief, no decadence, no shame, no disgrace—that I haven’t observed in our crimes, both yours and mine,”[3] Antigone laments to her sister Ismene. This stark English rendering becomes far more dramatic upon analyzing its Greek syntactical structure: the first use of the personal pronoun, ἐγὼ, is positioned just before the plural noun, κακῶν, the last word of the line, meaning “wicked deeds” or “crimes.” Placed at the end of the line, the two words are emphasized, isolated, and connected.


Even more fundamentally, the schism within the narrative derives from the circumstances of Polynices’ burial. Creon, the local sovereign, consigns his body to public decay. Antigone demands a ritual burial for her beloved brother. Creon refuses to relent. Antigone defies Creon’s directive and fulfills her familial obligation to bury the dead. All subsequent antagonisms stem from this dispute. This is the setting for the tumult of Antigone.


To trace the inner struggle of the play is to trace Antigone’s transition from citizen to unassimilable alien. The text provides disparate definitions of the citizen,[4] but none more thematically consequential than the one Creon delineates toward the climax of the play. Creon’s essentialist conception of citizenship is predicated on total obedience to authority. “There is no greater evil than civil disobedience,”[5] his credo reads. In Creon’s canon of justice, political injunction supersedes divine law. The same relation holds true for personal grievances and objectivity—the former supersedes the latter. Antigone’s insistence on honoring her familial due, while consistent with the overarching scheme of the divine, violates her covenant with the state and the sovereign. Once her ties to the state are formally broken, Antigone forfeits her status as member of the polis.


As Antigone loses her claim to citizenship, Creon rejects the democratic ideal and embraces a vision of empire. A new binary is introduced: democrat and imperialist. Antigone, apart from being ungovernable, is soon identified as a foreign opponent, subject to empire. Her location, too, is forcibly changed, underscoring her extrication from culture.


The reasons for Creon’s conduct are confused. Creon’s punishment of Antigone seems to waver between a defense of character, an assertion of sovereignty, a capitulation to authoritarian passions, and the product of egotism. These may all be compatible, but do not together constitute an ideology. His politics are, instead, hubris. As such, he is condemned to catastrophe.[6]


The struggle between internal and external forces within Antigone—whether between Creon and Antigone, empire and vassal, state and individual, human and divine, or family and polis—is ultimately lethal. Cultural conquest is deemed corrosive according to the design of the gods.
