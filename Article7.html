Definition and Example

by Zachary Groz

History 9/14/17

In Plato’s Meno, a number of questions of considerable pedagogical import are raised—some concerning the core characteristics of a productive student, others contending with the implications of language and necessity of participation, and still others regarding the struggle between form and content in argumentation. These philosophical inquiries, though they may be distinct from one another, concentrate on a single, central topic: the distinction between example and definition.


The essential question of Plato’s Meno is:

ἆρα διδακτὸν ἡ ἀρετή; ἢ οὐ διδακτὸν ἀλλ᾽ ἀσκητόν; ἢ οὔτε ἀσκητὸν οὔτε μαθητόν, ἀλλὰ φύσει παραγίγνεται τοῖς ἀνθρώποις ἢ ἄλλῳ τινὶ τρόπῳ;

whether excellence may be taught, or if it cannot be taught, whether it may be obtained through repetition. Or if it cannot be obtained through repetition or study, whether it is innate or it comes to humanity from some other source.


The preliminary statement of this question assumes an understanding of the essence of ἀρετή. It considers the means through which excellence may be obtained, but fails to deal with its abstract nature. Though Meno begins his analysis by searching for a possible paradigm for excellence, his theoretical approach remains fundamentally flawed, as does the framing of his initial question. In asking for examples of various forms of excellence, Meno neglects to address the definition of excellence itself—a fault which pervades the totality of his argument.


When asked later on to provide an explicit definition of ἀρετή, Meno replies:

πρῶτον μέν, εἰ βούλει ἀνδρὸς ἀρετήν, ῥᾴδιον, ὅτι αὕτη ἐστὶν ἀνδρὸς ἀρετή, ἱκανὸν εἶναι τὰ τῆς πόλεως πράττειν, καὶ πράττοντα τοὺς μὲν φίλους εὖ ποιεῖν, τοὺς δ᾽ ἐχθροὺς κακῶς, καὶ αὐτὸν εὐλαβεῖσθαι μηδὲν τοιοῦτον παθεῖν. εἰ δὲ βούλει γυναικὸς ἀρετήν, οὐ χαλεπὸν διελθεῖν, ὅτι δεῖ αὐτὴν τὴν οἰκίαν εὖ οἰκεῖν, σῴζουσάν τε τὰ ἔνδον καὶ κατήκοον οὖσαν τοῦ ἀνδρός.[2]

first if you desire that I relate the excellence of a man, it’ll be done easily. A man’s distinction is this: that he be able to manage the affairs of his city, that he do well by his friends and wickedly by those he despises, and to ensure that he suffer nothing himself. If you desire that I speak of the virtue of a woman, it’s also not difficult to describe. It is necessary for her to take care of the home, preserving the things within it, and to be subservient to her husband.


These descriptions of assorted types of excellence are simply examples and do not together constitute a rigorous definition. In this formulation, ἀρετή is reduced to an adjectival form—“it is excellent to perform a particular task”—as opposed to a concept. It’s meaning is effectively debased by example. A precise definition would disregard precedents and anecdotes, leaving behind only raw theory. Grounded in past observations, Meno’s argument lacks the self-containment necessary for it to be accurate and universally applicable. His thesis, likewise, is devoid of inference, an integral step toward discovery—it states opinion or “fact” and does nothing more to advance the explorative process. A clear definition of ἀρετή, therefore, can provide the basis for example. An example of ἀρετή, however, cannot provide a common definition for its many forms.

2. Meno, 71e1-e7.
